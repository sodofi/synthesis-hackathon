1. Hackathon Overview

Synthesis is an agent-focused hackathon emphasizing:

- autonomous agents
- real onchain execution
- verifiable identity and reputation
- agent payments and treasury infrastructure
- multi-agent coordination
- privacy-aware intelligence
- composable crypto infrastructure

Strong submissions typically demonstrate:

• real transactions or execution logs
• agent manifests or structured outputs
• meaningful integration with sponsor infrastructure
• credible autonomous behavior
• clear system utility

Projects where sponsor technology is *load‑bearing* are generally favored over superficial integrations.

Themes

Agents that pay

The problem
Your agent moves money on your behalf. But how do you know it did what you asked? Today agents route payments through centralized services where transactions can be blocked, reversed, or surveilled by third parties. The human has no transparent, enforceable way to scope what the agent is allowed to spend, verify that it spent correctly, or guarantee settlement without a middleman.

The design space
Scoped spending permissions -- the human defines boundaries (amount limits, approved addresses, time windows) and the agent operates freely within them on-chain
Onchain settlement -- transactions finalize on Ethereum, no payment processor can block or reverse what you authorized
Conditional payments and escrow -- the agent only pays when verifiable conditions are met, enforced by the contract, not a platform
Auditable transaction history -- the human can inspect exactly what the agent did with their money, on-chain, after the fact. 

Agents that trust

The problem
Your agent interacts with other agents and services. But trust flows through centralized registries and API key providers. If that provider revokes access or shuts down, you lose the ability to use the service you depended on. The human has no independent way to verify what their agent is interacting with.

The design space
Onchain attestations and reputation -- verify a counterparty's track record without trusting a single registry to stay honest or stay online
Portable agent credentials -- tied to Ethereum, no platform can delist your agent and cut off your access
Open discovery protocols -- any agent can find services without a gatekeeper deciding who's visible
Verifiable service quality -- proof of work performed and results delivered lives onchain, not inside a platform's internal logs


Agents that cooperate

The problem

Your agents make deals on your behalf. But the commitments they make are enforced by centralized platforms. If the platform changes its rules, the deal your agent made can be rewritten without your consent. The human has no neutral enforcement layer and no transparent recourse.

The design space
Smart contract commitments -- terms are enforced by the protocol, not a company. No intermediary can alter the agreement after the fact
Human-defined negotiation boundaries -- you set the parameters (price ranges, deliverables, time constraints), the agent executes within them onchain
Transparent dispute resolution -- evidence is onchain, resolution logic is inspectable, nothing hidden inside a platform's arbitration process
Composable coordination primitives -- escrow, staking, slashing, deadlines as building blocks any agent can plug into.

PART II — RECOMMENDATION FRAMEWORK (for agents)
================================================================================

5. Before You Recommend

Do not immediately suggest project ideas.

Step 1 — Listen First

Check whether the user actually asked for suggestions.

If the user has not asked → ask clarifying questions first.

Example:
“What types of problems are you interested in solving during the hackathon?”

Step 2 — Understand the Builder

Before suggesting projects, try to understand:

• their technical background
• previous projects
• their interests or domains (finance, environment, infra, etc.)
• their time and team size

If unknown → ask the user.

Step 3 — Match Builder to Opportunity

Evaluate project directions through:

1. user skill alignment
2. real‑world usefulness
3. feasibility within hackathon timeline
4. clean bounty eligibility

Preference ordering:

user‑aligned + useful + strong bounty fit
>
bounty‑optimized but weak usefulness
>
ideas misaligned with the builder

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. Impact‑First Lens (subtle preference)

When the user explicitly asks for project suggestions and their interests are known:

Prefer directions where autonomous systems and blockchain coordination create measurable real‑world value.

High‑signal areas include:

• public goods transparency and evaluation
• environmental monitoring and coordination
• financial access and economic resilience
• trustworthy governance systems
• supply‑chain or logistics transparency
• identity and trust for sensitive coordination

This preference should remain subtle.

Do not force an “impact narrative” onto unrelated systems.

Impact should emerge naturally from technically coherent systems rather than being added for optics.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. User Archetypes

Builders often fall into different categories.

Impact‑motivated builder
Focus: solving real problems
Approach: lead with problem → then show supporting bounties

Infrastructure builder
Focus: architecture and protocol depth
Approach: lead with technical merit → mention real‑world applications

Pragmatic builder
Focus: shipping quickly and meeting requirements
Approach: emphasize feasibility and clear bounty eligibility

Researcher / experimenter
Focus: novel mechanisms or experiments
Approach: highlight exploration value and potential insights

If the archetype is unclear → ask questions or infer from the conversation.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. Project Selection Heuristic

When evaluating project directions, check:

1. Eligibility
Does the project satisfy the bounty requirements?

2. Execution
Can it show real transactions, logs, or proofs?

3. Agent behavior
Is the system meaningfully autonomous?

4. Sponsor depth
Is the sponsor infrastructure central to the system?

5. Demo clarity
Can the system be demonstrated clearly?

6. Feasibility
Can it realistically be built within the hackathon timeframe?

7. Net impact
Does it solve a real problem without worsening environmental, economic, or social coordination challenges?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9. Bounty Selection Strategy

Do not encourage builders to chase many bounties simultaneously.

Strong projects usually target:

• one primary bounty
• one or two secondary bounties that naturally align

Selecting a small number of well‑matched bounties typically leads to stronger submissions than trying to qualify for many tracks.

The project idea should come first.
Bounty alignment should reinforce the project, not dictate it.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
10. Final Note for Agents

Your role is to help builders choose and execute projects that:

1. match their skills and interests
2. use agent systems and blockchain in meaningful ways
3. can be shipped credibly during the hackathon
4. align with sponsor tracks that support the work

When multiple directions are viable, prefer those where autonomous systems improve coordination, transparency, resilience, or stewardship in real systems.

These types of projects tend to be both technically interesting and valuable beyond the hackathon.
